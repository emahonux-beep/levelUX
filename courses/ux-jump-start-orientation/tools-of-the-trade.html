Tools of the Trade: What Every UX Designer Should Know

schedule 5 min read

As a UX designer, you'll wear a lot of hats — researcher, facilitator, storyteller, strategist. The tools below won't do the thinking for you, but knowing your way around them makes you a far more effective voice in the room. Here's what to have in your toolkit.

Pen and Paper (Yes, Really)

Whether it's digital or physical, pen and paper is your best friend. Before you open any app, start by jotting down ideas, sketching things out, and gathering your thoughts. Low-fidelity, fast, and judgment-free — nothing beats it for getting the earliest version of an idea out of your head and onto a page.

Whiteboarding Tools

Tools like FigJam, Miro, and Mural are built for this next stage. They're great for mapping user journeys, building diagrams, running workshops, and kicking off projects with a team. Think of them as the digital extension of your notebook — but built for collaboration.

Design Software

Figma is the best in the business, in my opinion. It's a true jack-of-all-trades — wireframing, high-fidelity UI design, prototyping, and design systems all in one place. If you only master one piece of software, make it this one.

AI Tools

AI tools can seriously accelerate your workflow — whether that's data analysis, creating decks, mocking up prototypes, or shipping directly to code. I personally prefer Claude, but tools like Cursor and Lovable work well too. The key is finding the one that fits how you think and work, and learning to use it as a collaborator, not just a shortcut.

Analytics Tools

Get fluent in tools like Adobe Analytics, Google Analytics, Databricks, and Verint so you can analyze experiences live in production. These tools help you measure success and pinpoint areas for improvement — turning your design decisions from opinions into evidence.

Your Voice

Bring your voice and your point of view into the room. You may be the only one advocating for the end user in a given conversation — it's on you to make sure they're heard.

Your Thinking Cap and Creativity

UX designers work with complex problems, ambiguity, and people who show up with big (sometimes wild) ideas. Part of the job is knowing how to prod and ask the right questions to figure out what's really being asked, what problem is actually being solved, and how to untangle the mess in the room.

lightbulb The bottom line

The tools don't make the designer, but knowing your way around them — and knowing when to reach for which one — is what makes you a genuinely effective voice at the table. It's all strategic, and it starts with mastering the basics.
