Collaboration & Relationships: The Human Side of UX

schedule 6 min read

Great design doesn't happen in isolation. As a UX designer, how well you collaborate — and the relationships you build along the way — can matter just as much as the work itself. This module covers what good collaboration actually looks like, and how to build the kind of relationships that make it possible.

Collaboration

Collaboration can look a ton of different ways. Sometimes it's as simple as sharing your work with other designers for feedback — early and often. Asking for comments, setting up time, sending the email. These are easy, low-effort ways to get others involved and make people feel heard.

There are also strategies you can use to pull thoughts out of your partners:

  • Ask questions early and often. Don't wait until a review is "polished" to invite input — the earlier people weigh in, the more useful their perspective is.
  • Sketch in real time. If you're having trouble understanding what someone means, start sketching it out live on the canvas as they talk. It gives them something concrete to react to and often surfaces misunderstandings faster than words alone.
  • Talk it out. Ask what's working and what isn't. A simple, direct question can unlock feedback that a polished deck or silent Figma comment never will.

Collaboration isn't a phase of the process — it's a habit you build into every stage of it.

Building Relationships

Collaboration works best when people feel like they can actually talk to you. That starts with fostering good relationships — get to know folks outside of the work itself, and look for ways to support others, not just lean on them for feedback.

If you feel disconnected from someone, don't ignore it — work on the relationship. That can be hard, but having the hard conversation is often a sign of good faith. It shows you care enough to repair the bridge instead of letting it stay broken.

I'll be honest: I've personally struggled with this. Having hard conversations, or feeling disconnected from people I work with, doesn't come naturally to me either. But taking the small steps — making the time, showing up, checking in — is what starts to bring people (and their ideas) together.

It means getting outside your comfort zone and meeting people where they are. Relationships are just as important as any tool in your kit, and how you talk to people can make or break how well you're able to do the work at all.

Persuasion

Collaboration isn't just about gathering input — it's also about knowing how to pitch your ideas once you've got them. That's where data, research, and analytics come in. Used well, they're not just proof points; they're the raw material for a story.

Painting a picture and giving people the "why" behind a decision is often what actually persuades folks to get behind an experience — even when they weren't aligned to begin with. A number on a slide rarely moves anyone. A number wrapped in context, framed around a real user problem, usually does.

There's also an art to how you present your recommendation:

  • Don't overload people with choices. Too many options can stall a decision instead of driving one.
  • Lead with your best-practice recommendation. Come in with a clear point of view, backed by your research and data.
  • Step back into alternatives when needed. If your original recommendation doesn't fully meet stakeholder needs, that's your cue to bring in other options — not to abandon the story, but to adapt it.

Persuasion isn't about winning an argument. It's about giving people enough context and confidence to say yes to the right thing.

lightbulb The bottom line

Collaboration is a skill, not a checkbox — and the relationships (and persuasion) behind it are what make it sustainable. Show up, make the time, tell the story, and meet people where they are.
