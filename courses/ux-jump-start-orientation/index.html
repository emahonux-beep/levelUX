Module 1 of 1

The people and the mode of work you're in.

schedule 8 min read

This module covers the two things that trip people up most in their first few weeks: not knowing who to go to for what, and not knowing what kind of work they're supposed to be doing. Both are fixable with a little context.

Work through the sections in order if you're brand new, or jump straight to whatever you need. You can check off each section as you go — your progress saves automatically.

info How this module is organized

We start with the people (who's who and how to approach them), then move into the mode of work (discovery vs. delivery) — because knowing which one you're in changes everything about how you show up.

1 Who's Who

Here's a general map of the roles you'll work with most. Titles and scope vary by team, so treat this as a starting point and confirm specifics with your manager.

Product Manager (PM)

Owns the "what" and "why" — business goals, prioritization, and roadmap. Your main partner for understanding constraints and trade-offs. Loop them in early; they hate surprises at a launch review more than almost anything else.

Engineering Lead / Tech Lead

Owns technical feasibility and implementation. Bring them in during design, not just at handoff — they'll often spot a simpler (or impossible) path before you've sunk hours into a direction.

UX Researcher

Partners with you on understanding user needs. Good researchers will push back on assumptions — treat that as a gift, not an obstacle.

Content Designer / UX Writer

Owns the words: microcopy, error states, tone, terminology. Bring them in while you're still exploring layout — content often reshapes structure.

Design Manager / Lead

Your first stop for craft feedback, career conversations, and unblocking friction. Use 1:1s to flag problems before they become escalations.

Stakeholders / Leadership

Directors, VPs, or cross-functional leads who approve direction or budget. They care about outcomes and risk — frame your work in those terms, not pixels.

2 Delivery vs. Discovery

One of the most useful distinctions to internalize early is what mode of work you're actually in — because it changes everything: your deliverables, your timeline, and what "done" looks like.

🔍 Discovery / Strategic Design

The goal is to reduce uncertainty about a problem before committing to a solution. Common activities: research synthesis, problem framing, journey mapping, early concepting, competitive analysis. Deliverables are often rough, exploratory, and disposable — the point is learning, not polish.

🚀 Delivery

The goal is to ship a well-defined solution. Common activities: high-fidelity design, prototyping, accessibility and dev handoff annotation, QA. Deliverables need to be precise and production-ready.

lightbulb Why this matters

If you bring delivery-level polish to a discovery conversation, you'll get feedback on pixels instead of the idea — and stakeholders may assume the decision is already made. If you bring discovery-level looseness to a delivery review, you'll create confusion about what's actually being built.

Naming which mode you're in at the start of a meeting ("this is early exploration" vs. "this is ready for sign-off") sets expectations and saves everyone time.
